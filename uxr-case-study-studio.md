# UXR Case Study Studio

A guided workflow for building interview-ready UX research case studies and writing strong CV bullets.

This is not a template to rush through. It is a thinking companion. Expect the user to pause, rewrite, and question their own answers. That is part of the work.

---



## Instructions

### Step 1: Determine What the User Has

Ask the user what they're starting with. Don't assume — ask directly:

**"What do you have so far? You can:**
- **Upload documents** (notes, decks, reports, Notion pages, a rough draft)
- **Write a quick blurb** about the project (a few sentences is fine)
- **Start from zero** — just tell me the project existed and we'll build it together"

Also ask: **"Is this a case study or CV bullets?"**

Route to the appropriate workflow:
- If case study with material → Step 2 → Step 3A
- If case study from scratch → Step 2 → Step 3B
- If CV bullets → jump to the CV Bullets section below

### Step 2: Choose Voice

Before writing anything, ask the user to pick their voice:

**"Which voice feels right for your case study?"**

1. **Conversational & human** — Reads like you're talking to a smart colleague over coffee. Uses "I" naturally, lets personality show, comfortable with imperfection. Good for portfolio sites and presentation-style case studies.

2. **Formal & structured** — Clean, precise, third-person-friendly. Organized with clear headers and measured language. Good for enterprise roles, academic-adjacent positions, or when the company culture leans formal.

3. **Confident & bold** — Short sentences. Strong verbs. No hedging. Every claim lands with evidence. Good for senior/staff roles where you need to signal authority and decisiveness.

Apply the chosen voice consistently across all sections. If the user changes their mind mid-way, offer to rewrite earlier sections to match.

### Step 3A: Map Existing Material (Gap Analysis)

When the user provides material, do NOT start rewriting immediately. First, map what they have against the 13-section structure:

**The 13 Sections:**
1. Context and background
2. Your role
3. Timeline
4. Research statement and goals
5. Research methodology
6. Recruitment criteria and process
7. Sample questions asked or usability tasks
8. Analysis and synthesis process
9. Outputs/deliverables
10. Impact
11. Next steps and recommendations
12. Reflections
13. (Optional) Biases and assumptions

**Present the gap analysis as a table:**

| # | Section | Status | Notes |
|---|---------|--------|-------|
| 1 | Context and background | ✅ Covered / ⚠️ Weak / ❌ Missing | Brief note on what's there or what's missing |

For each section, assess:
- **✅ Covered** — Content exists and is specific, shows reasoning, includes numbers where relevant
- **⚠️ Weak** — Content exists but is vague, generic, missing impact, or just reports events without showing thinking
- **❌ Missing** — Not addressed at all

After showing the table, say: **"Here's what I found. I'll help you strengthen the weak sections and fill the gaps. Want to go through them in order, or jump to a specific one?"**

Then for each weak or missing section:
1. Show what's there (if anything) and why it's weak — use the strong/weak examples from the Section Prompts Reference below to calibrate
2. Ask the guided "Write it out" prompts for that section
3. If the user gives a rough blurb, rewrite it in their chosen voice
4. If the user can't answer, help them think through it with the follow-up prompts
5. Show the rewritten section and get approval before moving on

### Step 3B: Build From Scratch

When the user has nothing written:

1. Ask them to give you a quick overview of the project — even 2-3 sentences is enough to start. Prompt: **"Tell me about the project in a few sentences. What company, what problem, what did you do, what happened?"**

2. From their response, identify which sections you can partially draft and which need more information

3. Walk through each section in order (1-13). For each:
   - Explain briefly what this section needs (one sentence)
   - Ask the "Write it out" prompts from the Section Prompts Reference below
   - When the user responds (even roughly), write the section in their chosen voice
   - Show it to them before moving on

Don't try to write the whole case study from a 3-sentence blurb. Each section needs the user's real input — the skill's job is to ask the right questions and shape the answers into strong writing.

### Step 4: Name, Tag, and Compile

Once all sections are drafted, finalise the case study with metadata and quality checks.

**4a. Propose a case study name**

Based on the content, propose 2-3 name options. Good names are specific and signal what the reader will learn — not just "Case Study 1."

Good names:
- "Shifting from Validation to Discovery at [Company]"
- "How JTBD Research Reframed Our Product Strategy"
- "Building a Research Practice from Zero"
- "Redesigning Onboarding Through Developer Research"

Bad names:
- "Case Study 1"
- "Research Project"
- "My Work at [Company]"

**4b. Add tags**

Add two types of tags under the case study name:

**Skill tags** (pick all that apply):
- Leadership & strategy
- Research skills
- Research ops & tools
- Research methods

**Methods tags** — extract from the methodology section the specific methods used in this case study. Examples:
- Generative interviews
- Usability testing
- Diary study
- Survey
- Concept testing
- Journey mapping
- Persona creation
- JTBD
- Card sorting
- Contextual inquiry
- A/B testing
- Service blueprint
- Affinity mapping
- Stakeholder workshops

Present the tags to the user: **"Here are the tags I'd suggest based on your case study. Want to change any?"**

**4c. Compile and quality check**

1. Compile into a single document with name + tags at the top (markdown by default, docx if requested)
2. Run the quality checklist from the Quality Checklist below
3. Flag any remaining issues
4. Present the complete case study
5. Ask: **"How does this feel? Anything that doesn't sound like you, or any section you want to revisit?"**

## CV Bullets

When the user asks for CV bullet help, read the CV Bullet Formula Reference below for the full formula, strong/weak examples, and power verbs.

**The core formula:**

```
[Power verb] + [what you did] + [quantifier] + [consequence transition] + [consequence]
```

**Workflow:**

1. If the user provides existing bullets → evaluate each against the formula, show what's missing (quantifier? consequence? specificity?), then rewrite
2. If writing from scratch → ask: "What did you do, and what changed because of it?" Then apply the formula
3. Always show before/after when rewriting
4. Check for: no vague/obvious language, every claim quantified, impact tied to a concrete outcome

## Section Essentials

Not every project covers all 13 sections. Guide the user with this:

**Essential (never skip):**
- 1 Context and background
- 2 Your role
- 4 Research statement and goals
- 5 Research methodology
- 8 Analysis and synthesis process
- 9 Outputs/deliverables
- 10 Impact
- 12 Reflections

**Important (include if possible):**
- 3 Timeline
- 6 Recruitment criteria and process
- 11 Next steps and recommendations

**Can be brief:**
- 7 Sample questions or usability tasks

**Optional:**
- 13 Biases and assumptions

If the user wants to skip an essential section, push back gently: **"This section is one of the ones hiring managers look for specifically. Even a short version is better than skipping it. Want to give me a few sentences and I'll shape it?"**

## Troubleshooting

### User provides very little material
Don't invent details. Switch to the "Build From Scratch" flow (Step 3B) and ask the guided questions section by section. It's OK if this takes multiple turns.

### User's draft is mostly complete but weak
Don't rewrite everything at once. Show specific weak → strong rewrites for 2-3 sections first, so the user sees the pattern. Then ask: **"Want me to do this for every section?"**

### User wants to skip sections
Refer to the Section Essentials list above. Respect their choice on non-essential sections, push back gently on essential ones.

### User's material doesn't fit the structure
If the content is a presentation, Slack thread, or unstructured notes:
- Extract what maps to each section
- Show the gap analysis as normal
- Don't force content into wrong sections
- Say: **"Your notes cover X really well. For the other sections, I'll ask you some questions."**

### User is stuck on a section
If the user can't answer the prompts for a section:
- Try the "Examples across contexts" variants (B2B, B2C, Internal) from the section prompts
- Ask them to think about a specific moment rather than abstract descriptions
- Offer to come back to it after doing other sections — sometimes later sections jog memory

### User wants a different format
The default output is markdown. If the user asks for:
- **Notion** — use markdown with clean headers (Notion imports markdown well)
- **Presentation/deck** — suggest structuring as one section per slide, with the key point bolded
- **Docx** — generate as a Word document

## What Makes a Weak vs Strong Case Study

Embed this understanding in every section you write:

**Weak case studies:**
- Report what happened without showing reasoning
- Don't name collaborators or explain their roles
- Skip method choices — just say "we did interviews"
- Present findings without showing the synthesis process
- List outcomes without tying them to concrete decisions or metrics
- Read like a project log, not a story

**Strong case studies:**
- Show the thinking — why this method, why these participants, why this approach
- Name collaborators and their specific roles
- Explain synthesis step by step — how messy data became clear insight
- Connect every output to a concrete outcome, decision, or behaviour change
- Tell a story with a clear before → after arc
- Include honest reflections on what didn't work

Apply this lens to every section you write or rewrite. If a section reads like it could be about any project at any company, it's too vague.

# Section Prompts Reference

This file contains the guided prompts for each of the 13 case study sections. For each section you'll find:
- **What this section does** — why it matters to hiring managers
- **Guiding questions** — ask these if the user hasn't provided content
- **Write it out** — fill-in-the-blank prompts to draw out specifics
- **Strong examples** — what good looks like
- **Weak examples** — what to avoid and rewrite
- **Examples across contexts** — B2B, B2C, Internal variants

---

## 1. Context and Background

**What this section does:** Orients the reader so every decision later makes sense. Vague context forces readers to guess. This section should make someone unfamiliar with the company and project feel grounded in 30 seconds.

**Guiding questions:**
- Share something about yourself beyond your job title (a recent change, a hobby, something personal)
- What does the company do and who are its users?
- What was this project about, in plain language — no jargon?

**Write it out:**
- This company operates in the ___ space.
- The product or service helps users ___.
- The users involved in this work were ___.
- This project focused on ___.

**Strong examples:**
- "Hi, I'm Dragana. I'm a UX research lead based in Paris who's been doing and not writing about it enough. I worked at a headless CMS — a developer tool where developers discover, sign up, and build with the product self-serve."
- "A B2B SaaS tool that helps finance teams manage their invoicing workflow."
- "A consumer fitness app focused on helping people stick with daily habits."

**Weak examples:**
- "A tech startup."
- "A platform with many users."
- "I worked at a company doing research." (no specifics, no personality)

**Examples across contexts:**
- B2B: "Procurement software used by mid-size operations teams."
- B2C: "A subscription meal planning app."
- Internal: "An internal support ticket routing tool."

---

## 2. Your Role

**What this section does:** Shows your specific contribution, not just what the team did. Hiring managers want to know what YOU did versus what happened around you.

**Guiding questions:**
- What was your role on this project? Were you leading or contributing?
- Who else was involved, and how did you collaborate with them?
- How did this project get prioritised against other work?

**Write it out:**
- I was ___ on this project.
- I worked with ___ (name roles, not just "the team").
- My specific contribution was ___.
- The project was prioritised because ___.

**Strong examples:**
- "I was the only researcher, working alongside two designers, a PM, and a few developers. I led all the interviews, the end-to-end process — recruitment, synthesis, workshops. The project came from a roadmapping session I was part of, and I had stakeholders fill out an intake template to help me prioritise it."

**Weak examples:**
- "I was part of the research team." (what did YOU do?)
- "I helped with research." (too vague to assess)

---

## 3. Timeline

**What this section does:** Shows you can plan and scope work. Break it down into phases so it's concrete.

**Guiding questions:**
- How long did the project take overall?
- Can you break it into phases? (scoping, recruitment, fieldwork, analysis, reporting)
- Were there constraints or changes to the timeline?

**Write it out:**
- The project took ___ total.
- Broken down: scoping (___), recruitment (___), research (___), analysis (___), reporting (___).
- The main timeline constraint was ___.

**Strong examples:**
- "Six weeks total. Scoping: 1 week. Recruitment: 2 weeks. Conducting research: 2.5 weeks. Survey: 1 week. Analysis: 1.5 weeks. Reporting: 1 week."

**Weak examples:**
- "It took a few months." (too vague)
- "We worked on it for a while." (no signal about planning ability)

---

## 4. Research Statement and Goals

**What this section does:** Anchors the entire case study. A broad statement weakens everything after it. Name what you wanted to understand, who needed it, and the decision it supported.

**Guiding questions:**
- What were you trying to learn or understand?
- Where did this research question come from? (prior research, a stakeholder, a data signal?)
- Did you need buy-in? How did you get it?
- What decision would this research inform?

**Write it out:**
- We were trying to understand ___.
- For ___ (who needed the answer).
- So that ___ could decide ___.
- This goal existed because ___ was unclear.
- Without clarity here, the team would likely ___.

**Research statement template:**
"We wanted to better understand how users [think about / make decisions on / interact with] [subject] in order to [create / improve] [product/service]"

**Strong examples:**
- "We wanted to understand how people think about ordering food online and how they interact with our product, so we could improve the app experience."
- "Support volume went up but teams disagreed on the root cause."

**Weak examples:**
- "We wanted to learn more about our users."
- "Stakeholders asked for research."
- "It felt like a good time to explore."

**Examples across contexts:**
- B2B: "Sales data conflicted with what product analytics showed."
- B2C: "Drop-off increased after a recent release."
- Internal: "Escalations were rising without a clear pattern."

---

## 5. Research Methodology

**What this section does:** Shows reasoning, not just tool knowledge. Listing methods without explaining why adds nothing. Explain why the method fit the question and what it couldn't answer.

**Guiding questions:**
- What methods did you choose and why?
- What methods did you consider but not use?
- Were stakeholders present during the research? In what role?
- How many sessions did you run and how long were they?

**Write it out:**
- We chose ___ because it fit ___.
- This approach was limited in ___.
- We decided against ___ because it focused on ___.
- We combined ___ and ___ because ___.

**Strong examples:**
- "We chose one-on-one generative interviews — 90-minute conversations split between understanding mental models and walking through the product. I chose this because it gave us both the 'why' behind behaviour and observable pain points. We ran 17 interviews, then followed up with a survey of 1,000 users to validate and prioritise findings."
- "Moderated sessions let us probe the reasoning behind each decision."

**Weak examples:**
- "We used interviews because they're standard."
- "We did qualitative research." (which kind? why that kind?)

**Examples across contexts:**
- B2B: "Interviews to understand buying decision workflows."
- B2C: "Diary study to capture habitual behaviour over time."
- Internal: "Contextual inquiry to observe how the tool is actually used."

---

## 6. Recruitment Criteria and Process

**What this section does:** Explains who mattered and why. Don't default to sample size justification — explain who had the experience you needed and who didn't.

**Guiding questions:**
- Who did you recruit and why those people specifically?
- What were the screening criteria?
- How did you recruit them? (panel, tool, outreach, internal?)
- Did you offer incentives? Why or why not?
- What was difficult about recruitment?

**Write it out:**
- We needed participants who ___.
- We excluded ___ because ___.
- Timing mattered because ___.
- We recruited via ___.

**Strong examples:**
- "We recruited a mix of existing users and non-users to get fresh perspective. Criteria: had ordered food online at least once in the past month, age 25-45, English-speaking. We used an in-app pop-up and emailed newsletter subscribers. We offered a 20% discount voucher as incentive to recruit quickly."
- "We targeted users within their first two weeks of signing up."

**Weak examples:**
- "All users."
- "We recruited some participants." (no criteria, no reasoning)

---

## 7. Sample Questions or Usability Tasks

**What this section does:** Shows your craft as a researcher. Don't list every question — pick 3-5 that demonstrate your thinking and approach.

**Guiding questions:**
- What are some example questions from your guide?
- Why did you frame them that way?
- If usability: what tasks did you set? What were you measuring?
- What questioning technique did you use? (open-ended, laddering, etc.)

**Write it out:**
- I framed questions around ___ because ___.
- Example questions that show my approach: [list 3-5]
- For the walkthrough, we had participants ___.

**Strong examples:**
- "I used very open-ended questions to start with — asking participants to walk us through their last order, whether or not it was on our platform. For the second half, I observed them using the app while thinking aloud and asked clarifying questions at natural pauses."

**Weak examples:**
- "I asked about their experience." (which questions? why that framing?)
- [Listing 15 questions without explaining the thinking behind any of them]

---

## 8. Analysis and Synthesis Process

**What this section does:** This is where weak case studies fall apart. Hiring managers are listening for how you reason with ambiguity, how you decide what matters, and how your understanding changed during analysis.

**Guiding questions:**
- How did you go from raw data to insights?
- Did you debrief after each session? With whom?
- What coding or tagging approach did you use?
- Who else was part of synthesis?
- Was there a moment where your initial framing broke down?

**Write it out (describe the raw data):**
- At the start, the data looked like ___.
- I organised it by ___.
- The first patterns I noticed were ___.

**Write it out (show how your thinking changed):**
- As synthesis progressed, we noticed ___.
- This challenged our earlier assumption that ___.
- We shifted our focus from ___ to ___.

**Strong examples:**
- "After each session, I ran a 30-minute debrief with the team on key takeaways, surprises, and pain points. I transcribed each session within 24 hours into a spreadsheet, tagging data by pain points, needs, goals, and tools. Halfway through I ran a mini-synthesis with stakeholders — affinity mapping the top trends. This kept 17 sessions from feeling overwhelming."
- "We went in expecting one root cause. By session 8, the data pointed somewhere completely different."

**Weak examples:**
- "We coded the data and identified themes."
- "Themes evolved." / "New insights emerged." / "Our understanding deepened."

**Examples across contexts:**
- B2B: "We expected the bottleneck at procurement. It was actually at internal approval."
- B2C: "We assumed the problem was motivation. Users were motivated — they just thought missing one day meant failure."
- Internal: "Teams blamed the interface. Synthesis showed the issue was unclear ownership between teams."

---

## 9. Outputs/Deliverables

**What this section does:** Shows how insight moved into action. Don't just list deliverables — explain why that format, who received it, and what it enabled.

**Guiding questions:**
- What did you produce? (personas, journey maps, reports, recommendations?)
- Why did you choose those deliverables over alternatives?
- How did you share the research? (reports, presentations, video clips, Notion?)
- Who received them and how did they use them?

**Write it out:**
- We created ___ instead of ___.
- This helped the team ___.
- We shared via ___ because ___.

**Strong examples:**
- "I put together a session snapshot after each interview — a high-level, at-a-glance summary teams could scan in two minutes. After final synthesis, I delivered a full report with video clips, two new personas, and a first-iteration journey map. We presented to product, marketing, sales, and C-level."
- "A prioritised pain-point list with severity scores that the PM embedded directly in sprint planning."

**Weak examples:**
- "A report."
- "We did a presentation." (what was in it? who used it? what did it change?)

---

## 10. Impact

**What this section does:** This is the section hiring managers care about most. It shows what actually shifted as a result of your research. Don't just say "stakeholders liked it" — name what changed, for whom, and at what scale.

Use the **Impact Framework** below to go deep. Walk the user through both dimensions: **Impact Type** (what kind of change) and **Scale of Impact** (how far it reached).

### Impact Type Framework

Prompt the user on each type. Not every project will have all types — but pushing on each one often reveals impact the user forgot to mention.

**Product Impact:**
- Influenced product change (design change, problem fixed, feature built)
- Influenced product strategy (influenced quarterly/yearly goals, introduced new concept, blocked a launch)

**Cultural Impact:**
- Increased stakeholder exposure to users (new stakeholder participated in research)
- Shared in communications (cited by stakeholder in presentation, included in email)
- Prompted further research (new live experiment, new marketing effort, prioritised new UXR initiative)
- Prompted new collaboration (new cross-functional collaboration, triangulation of UXR data with other data)
- Elevated UXR status (got key stakeholder interested in UXR, research relied on at increasingly higher levels of leadership)

**Internal Impact:**
- Developed UXR infrastructure (established new method, developed new process)

### Scale of Impact

For each impact the user names, ask: **"How far did this reach?"**

| Scale | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Individual stakeholder | PM, UX, engineering partner |
| Individual project or team | Small group focused on one feature or element |
| Body of work | Group of projects or teams on a similar topic |
| Organisation | Higher-level grouping, usually led by a Director or VP |
| Company | Multiple organisations, highest levels of leadership |
| Beyond company | Industry influence, published work, conference talks |

### Guiding questions

- What changed because of your research — for the team, the org, the business?
- Who used the insights, and how did they use them?
- Were any business metrics or KPIs affected?
- What DIDN'T change — and is that also impact? (preventing wrong decisions counts)
- Did anyone cite your research in their own work? (presentations, emails, planning docs)
- Did your research lead to more research or experimentation?
- Did new cross-team collaborations start because of this work?
- Did leadership's engagement with research change?

### Write it out (product impact)
- The research changed ___ in the product/experience.
- Before, the team planned to ___. After, they ___.
- This reached the level of ___ (individual/team/body of work/org/company).

### Write it out (cultural impact)
- After this research, ___ (stakeholder/team) started ___.
- The findings were cited in ___.
- This prompted ___ (new research/collaboration/experiment).
- UXR status changed because ___.

### Write it out (internal impact)
- We established ___ (new method/process/infrastructure).
- This is still used today for ___.

### Write it out (if impact was preventing a wrong move)
- The research showed that ___ was not the problem.
- This prevented the team from ___.
- Without this work, the team would likely have ___.

### Strong examples
- "During quarterly planning, the personas and pain points were used to prioritise what to build next. Marketing iterated their content strategy, which increased conversion rate and orders per subscriber."
- "The culture shifted — the organisation moved to a more user-centric mindset. Research went from being tolerated to being expected." (Cultural impact — elevated UXR status, company scale)
- "The CTO cited the journey map in a board presentation. Two departments that had never worked together started a joint initiative based on the findings." (Cultural impact — shared in communications + prompted new collaboration)
- "We prevented a rebuild of a feature nobody was actually asking for. The research showed usage wasn't the problem — discoverability was." (Product impact — preventing a wrong move)

### Weak examples
- "The research was well received."
- "Stakeholders liked the findings." (so what? what changed?)
- "It had a big impact." (on what? for whom? at what scale?)

---

## 11. Next Steps and Recommendations

**What this section does:** Shows you think beyond the immediate deliverable. What came after? What would you recommend going forward?

**Guiding questions:**
- What happened after the research wrapped?
- What recommendations did you make to the team or org?
- How did findings feed into product or design changes?
- Was follow-up research planned?

**Write it out:**
- The immediate follow-up was ___.
- We recommended ___ because ___.
- The open questions remaining are ___.

**Strong examples:**
- "We identified several pain points and followed up with ideation and usability testing. We shipped an improved flow that increased click-through rate by 15%, and started a loyalty initiative that improved retention by 5%. The personas continue to be iterated as we learn more."

**Weak examples:**
- "We plan to do more research." (on what? when? why?)

---

## 12. Reflections

**What this section does:** Shows self-awareness and growth. Name one adjustment, one keeper, and one lesson. Honesty here signals maturity — not everything needs to have gone perfectly.

**Guiding questions:**
- What went well?
- What didn't go as well?
- What challenges came up?
- What would you do differently next time?

**Write it out:**
- If I ran this again, I would ___ earlier.
- One thing I would keep is ___.
- This project taught me ___.

**Strong examples:**
- "What went well: stakeholders actually participated, the org shifted toward user-centricity, and people used the research. Challenges: recruitment was slow — I'd target more precisely next time. Scope changed mid-project — I'd lock it down with an intake document sooner. A few last-minute no-shows meant scrambling for backups — I'd build backup participants into the recruitment plan from the start."

**Weak examples:**
- "I would not change anything." (no self-awareness signal)
- "Everything went great." (not believable)

---

## 13. (Optional) Biases and Assumptions

**What this section does:** Shows you understand your own lens and the team's. Especially powerful when assumptions were disproved by the research.

**Guiding questions:**
- What assumptions did you or the team bring in?
- Were any validated or disproved?
- How did you account for bias in recruitment, analysis, or how you framed the problem?

**Write it out:**
- We assumed ___.
- We validated that ___.
- We disproved that ___ — instead, ___.

**Strong examples:**
- "We assumed people used the product for convenience. Disproved: they used it when something went wrong (a forgotten ingredient, a stressful day), for fun (a relaxed night in), not out of laziness. We validated that people want multiple options, but they tend to stick to a few favourites."
- "I assumed long-time users would have the most useful feedback. Turns out newer users caught friction the rest of us had learned to work around."

**Weak examples:**
- "We tried to be unbiased." (how? what did you actually check?)
- "We were aware of our assumptions." (which ones?)

# CV Bullet Formula Reference

## The Formula

Every bullet point follows this structure — no exceptions:

```
[Power verb] + [what you did] + [quantifier] + [consequence transition] + [consequence]
```

If a bullet doesn't have a quantifier or consequence, it's not done yet.

---

## Before/After Examples

### Example 1
**Bad:** Assisted product managers in prioritising new features/improvements on product roadmaps
**Good:** Created and leveraged three personas to prioritise the feature roadmap, and empowered product managers and developers to write customer-centric user stories
**Why:** Bad version says "assisted" without explaining how. Good version names the work (personas), the count (three), and the consequence (customer-centric user stories).

### Example 2
**Bad:** Collaborated with designers to drive major product iterations through usability testing
**Good:** Conducted over 75 usability tests, which resulted in actionable insights, increasing customer satisfaction scores (SUS) from 46 (below average) to 71 (above average), and increased overall task completion by 23%
**Why:** Numbers (75 tests, SUS 46→71, 23% task completion) make the claim verifiable and concrete.

### Example 3
**Bad:** Evangelised the user's perspective through the creation of user personas, empathy maps, journey maps, and user scenarios
**Good:** Used our service blueprint to inform sales strategy, and reduce client implementation timeline by 50%, from six months to two to three months
**Why:** Bad version tries to cram everything in and dilutes the point. Good version picks one deliverable, one consequence, with a specific number.

### Example 4
**Bad:** Created and shared insights in creative ways throughout the product team
**Good:** Used creative sharing methods such as usability bingo and opening night, which aided in executive buy-in and led to a 20% increase in the annual research budget
**Why:** Names the specific methods (usability bingo, opening night) and ties to a concrete impact (budget increase).

### Example 5
**Bad:** Educated stakeholders on the value of research and democratised usability testing through training
**Good:** Developed over 20 user research templates and curriculum to teach 10+ colleagues how to conduct usability sessions, leading to a 30/70 split in evaluative and generative research
**Why:** Quantified (20 templates, 10+ colleagues) and shows the shift in research mix as the consequence.

### Example 6
**Bad:** Built a research framework and practice for end-to-end research projects
**Good:** Created a user research structure and framework, which placed user research within an agile environment and reduced wasted development time by 45%
**Why:** "Built a framework" says nothing about what it enabled. The good version names the environment (agile) and the impact (45% reduction).

---

## What Makes a Bad Bullet

- Obvious tasks any researcher should do ("conducted qualitative research")
- Copy-pasted from job descriptions
- No numbers at all
- No consequence (no "so what?")
- Fluffy and vague ("evangelised the user's perspective")
- Too broad ("managed end-to-end research")
- Tries to cram everything into one bullet

---

## What Makes a Good Bullet

- Specific about what you did and how
- Quantified (studies, stakeholders, teams, templates, %)
- Tied to a concrete consequence or outcome
- Unique to you — not generic research tasks
- Narrow scope — one clear point per bullet

---

## Power Verbs for UXR

Use these instead of "managed," "helped," "assisted":

**Research:** Conducted, Mapped, Evaluated, Assessed, Investigated, Benchmarked, Triangulated
**Building:** Created, Designed, Developed, Established, Architected, Built, Launched
**Leadership:** Led, Orchestrated, Spearheaded, Championed, Drove, Shaped, Defined
**Enablement:** Coached, Trained, Mentored, Enabled, Empowered, Equipped
**Strategy:** Prioritised, Aligned, Informed, Influenced, Positioned, Steered
**Ops:** Standardised, Streamlined, Migrated, Scaled, Automated, Reduced
**Communication:** Presented, Advocated, Evangelised, Facilitated, Articulated

---

## Finding Numbers When You Think You Don't Have Any

Ask yourself:
- How many studies/interviews/tests?
- How many stakeholders/teams/departments?
- How many templates/tools/processes created?
- What % improvement (time, satisfaction, coverage)?
- What was the before → after state?
- How many people trained/coached/hired?
- What budget/headcount/scope change resulted?

---

## Consequence Transitions

Use these to connect the action to the impact:
- "which resulted in..."
- "leading to..."
- "contributing to..."
- "enabling..."
- "reducing... by..."
- "increasing... by..."
- "informing..."
- "shaping..."

# Quality Checklist

Run this checklist before presenting a completed case study or set of CV bullets to the user. Flag any items that don't pass.

---

## Case Study Quality Checklist

### Structure
- [ ] All essential sections present (1, 2, 4, 5, 8, 9, 10, 12)
- [ ] Sections flow logically — a reader unfamiliar with the project can follow
- [ ] No section is just a list of items without explanation
- [ ] Context section makes the company and project clear in under 30 seconds

### Specificity
- [ ] Every section shows reasoning, not just reporting ("we chose X because Y")
- [ ] Collaborators are named with their roles (not just "the team")
- [ ] Method choices are explained (why this method, why not alternatives)
- [ ] Numbers are present where possible (sessions, participants, stakeholders, %, timelines)
- [ ] No jargon left unexplained — someone outside UXR can follow

### Synthesis Section (the one that matters most)
- [ ] Describes what raw data looked like at the start
- [ ] Shows the steps from data → codes → themes → insights
- [ ] Includes at least one moment where initial framing broke or thinking shifted
- [ ] Names who was involved in synthesis
- [ ] Does NOT just say "we identified themes" or "patterns emerged"

### Impact Section
- [ ] Ties outcomes to concrete decisions, behaviour changes, or metrics
- [ ] Addresses at least two of: product impact, decision-maker impact, ways-of-working impact
- [ ] If impact was preventing a wrong move, that's named explicitly
- [ ] Does NOT just say "stakeholders liked it" or "the research was well received"

### Voice & Tone
- [ ] Consistent with the user's chosen voice throughout
- [ ] Reads like a person wrote it, not a template filled in
- [ ] Personal introduction (section 1) includes something human, not just job title
- [ ] Reflections (section 12) are honest, not just "everything went great"

### Completeness
- [ ] No placeholder text remaining
- [ ] Artifact/image placeholders marked with 📎 where visuals should go
- [ ] All claims could be defended in an interview ("tell me more about that")

---

## CV Bullet Quality Checklist

- [ ] Every bullet follows the formula: power verb + what + quantifier + consequence
- [ ] No fluffy or obvious language ("end-to-end research process" alone = weak)
- [ ] Every claim is quantified where possible
- [ ] No bullets start with "Helped" or "Assisted" or "Participated in"
- [ ] Each bullet makes one clear point (not everything crammed together)
- [ ] Consequences are concrete (not "improved the product" — how? by how much?)
- [ ] No bullets could apply to any researcher at any company — each is specific to this person's work

## Common Issues to Flag

**"This could be anyone's case study"**
If you replace the company name and it still reads the same, the content is too generic. Push for specific details, names, numbers, and decisions.

**"Lots of what, no why"**
Sections that list activities without explaining reasoning. For every "we did X," there should be a "because Y" or "which led to Z."

**"The synthesis black box"**
Data goes in, insights come out, nothing in between. This is the #1 weakness in case studies. Push for the messy middle.

**"Impact without attribution"**
Claiming business metrics improved without showing the connection to the research. It's OK to say "contributed to" — but explain the chain.

**"Reflections that aren't reflections"**
"Everything went well" or pure positives. Real reflections name what was hard, what you'd change, what surprised you.
